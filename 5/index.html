Reading the map.

The three that exited sit top-left: neutral-ish hosts, but pure ‘rent the box’ with no durable service layer. Rebel Foods & Curefoods sit low — operator-first, running their own brands, so they can never credibly be a neutral landlord for rivals.

The top-right — neutral host plus a real compliance/data layer — is uncontested. That is the investable idea. It is also, honestly, the corner three funded giants aimed for and couldn’t hold.

Chapter 6 · The Human Number

The number a home chef feels — and the one that keeps you honest.

The whole model rests on a single relatable gap: what it costs Priya to go it alone versus what a hub charges her. That gap is her reason to come. Whether it’s enough to keep you alive is a harder number.

The price gap is the tenant’s reason to switch — rent, equipment, licensing and shared staff bundled below the DIY cost of a worse setup. But the membership only wins if the hub stays near-full: the break-even model shows a single 20-station hub needs close to 100% occupancy at premium pricing just to cover fixed costs. Occupancy, and the ancillary compliance/data layers, are the operator’s only real reason to survive — not the rent line alone.
