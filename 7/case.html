Section 01

Executive Summary

India's affluent run their homes on a workforce sourced through WhatsApp groups and watchmen, vetted by nobody, accountable to no one — and they know it.

Managed Household Staff is a Mumbai-first business built on a single unoccupied box in a crowded market: premium + genuinely trained + actively managed + guaranteed. It recruits, background-verifies, trains, places and then keeps managing — payroll, supervision, quality control, compliance and a service-level replacement guarantee — the cooks, nannies, drivers, estate managers, housekeepers and butlers that HNI and UHNI households depend on. Where the entire existing market treats staffing as a one-time match and then disappears, this is a recurring managed-service retainer: the branded, accountable layer over an enormous, chaotic, cash-based informal market.

The problem is trust, and it is documented. Fewer than 10% of families do any proper due-diligence before letting a stranger live in their home (OnGrid); most rely on a photocopied ID and a verbal reference. Mumbai Police runs a dedicated online domestic-servant verification form precisely because theft and misconduct are live concerns — yet almost no one uses it. And the category's own scaled leader, BookMyBai, carries a 1/5 rating on MouthShut, with 2025 complaints of fake profiles, staff who "can't cook," and replacements that never arrived. The trusted brand is trusted-but-poor-quality, which is exactly why the premium end sits wide open.

Why now. Mumbai concentrates 35.4% of all of India's ultra-high-net-worth individuals (US$30M+) and 92 US-dollar billionaires — Asia's billionaire capital (Knight Frank; Hurun). Female labour-force participation nearly doubled from 23.3% to 41.7% in six years, so fewer households have anyone home to run them. And capital is validating the category — over ₹1,200 crore of venture funding has poured into quick, gig, cheap house-help (Snabbit ~$112M, Pronto ~$40M) — while ignoring the premium managed niche entirely. Meanwhile the pending National Policy on Domestic Workers explicitly targets placement-agency regulation and model contracts, which favours a formal, compliant, DPDP-ready operator over the informal players it will squeeze.

The solution is a proprietary training academy feeding a certified, background-verified staff pool; an employer-of-record management layer that runs payroll, PF/ESIC and supervision; and a family app that turns an opaque, anxiety-ridden arrangement into a visible, accountable service — attendance, payslips, SLA-tracked replacement requests, QC ratings and a dedicated relationship manager. The strategic spine is a deliberate choice to employ (or employ-and-supervise) rather than merely broker staff: heavier on compliance, but that compliance is the moat and the ESG story.

Target customers are four personas — the multi-property Malabar Hill/Altamount Road UHNI family that needs a chief-of-staff layer; the returning-NRI family that already expects formal, contracted household staffing and is frustrated by India's informality; the time-starved dual-income HNI couple who want it simply done for them; and the new-money founder household scaling its lifestyle fast with no legacy staff network.

Key highlights: an estimatedEST. ₹1.33 crore seed requirement; a defensible Year-3 revenue targetEST. of ~₹12.5 crore at ~250 managed households; break-evenEST. at roughly 55–70 managed households (~Year 2 on a disciplined ramp); estimatedEST. LTV:CAC of 10–15:1 if retention holds; and a Mumbai → Delhi-NCR/Bengaluru → other-metros → GCC-diaspora expansion path. The single biggest risk is the informal-market undercut and direct-poaching loop — a family takes the trained staff and drops the retainer — which the entire moat design must break, and which the Mumbai pilot exists to prove.

Consumer & Decision-Maker Behaviour

This is a trust product for the top 1%, not a price product. The buyer is not comparing salary rates — the salary is pass-through. They are buying the removal of a specific, recurring anxiety: the bad hire, the sudden quit, the theft, the stranger who was never verified. Purchase is often triggered by a life event — a new home being furnished, a return from abroad, a sudden liquidity event, or a bad experience with an informal hire — so acquisition must be relationship-led and reach the household at that moment, through the designers, private banks and brokers already present in their lives.

Section 04

Macro & Regulatory

The defining strategic fork is EMPLOY vs. BROKER. It changes which laws bind the business, the GST base, and the entire cost structure. The recommended lane is employer-of-record / managed (with a hybrid option at the lower tier): heavier compliance — EPF/ESIC/gratuity, GST-on-gross, minimum-wage attachment — but that compliance is the moat and the ESG story, and it is what makes the trust, control and guarantee real. This choice is held consistent across Financials and Business Model.

PESTLE Analysis

Political

Domestic work is politically "invisible" — no dedicated central statute. The National Policy on Domestic Workers has been in draft since ~2017 and remains un-notified; India has not ratified ILO Convention 189. The direction of travel, however, is toward formalisation.

Economic

18% GST applies to manpower-supply/recruitment/placement (SAC 99851 family). Employ-and-second means GST on the gross value (wages + margin); pure placement means GST on the fee only — a core cost-structure fork.

Social

Deep informality, stigma and near-zero registration mean a compliant, verified, career-track offering is a genuine differentiator built on near-absent formal infrastructure — and dual-income households increasingly need it.

Technological

e-Shram (the national unorganised-worker database) is the registration rail if staff are positioned as gig/unorganised; BGV vendors (AuthBridge, IDfy, OnGrid) provide the verification stack.

Legal

A patchwork: obligations arise from generic labour, social-security, data-protection, child-labour and tax statutes rather than one domestic-worker law (detailed below).

Environmental

Not materially applicable to the operating model.

Government Regulations & Licensing

  • No dedicated domestic-worker law; a draft National Policy is coming — it would let workers register as unorganised workers, set minimum-wage/social-security access, and crucially regulate placement agencies and mandate a model contract. A compliant operator is the intended survivor of that regime.
  • Code on Social Security, 2020 — in force from 21 Nov 2025 — the employ-vs-broker decision point. Broker via an app/marketplace likely makes the business an "aggregator" liable for a 1–2%-of-turnover Social Security Fund contribution, with workers as e-Shram-registered gig workers, not employees. Employing the staff makes the business an employer, with EPF/ESIC, gratuity and full statutory liabilities once thresholds are met — heavier, but premium and defensible.
  • Maharashtra Domestic Workers Welfare Board Act, 2008 — exists but is largely dormant; registers workers (18–60) as welfare beneficiaries. Unions petitioned in 2025 to revive it.
  • Unorganised Workers' Social Security Act, 2008 — expressly covers domestic workers within "wage worker."
  • Minimum Wages / Code on Wages — Maharashtra has no notified minimum wage for domestic workers (only ~7 states do), but if the business employs staff via a registered establishment, general Maharashtra minimum-wage logic attaches to its employees.
  • Background verification + DPDP Act 2023 — police verification is imposed via city/state rules and on placement agencies (e.g. the Delhi Private Placement Agencies Order 2014 mandates licensing + proprietor police-verification, with a ₹50,000 penalty for operating unlicensed). Handling staff and HNI PII makes the business a Data Fiduciary under DPDP 2023 — consent-based BGV, a BGV vendor as Data Processor, security safeguards, breach notification, and penalties up to ₹250 crore.
  • Maharashtra Shops & Establishments Act, 2017 — governs the office/company (registration mandatory at 10+ workers), binding the Mumbai HQ, not the private worksite.
  • Trafficking & child-labour red lines — under the Child & Adolescent Labour (Prohibition & Regulation) Act 1986 (2016 amendment), under-14s are barred from all work and child domestic work is a notified hazardous occupation; adolescents (14–18) are barred from it too. Placement agencies are a known trafficking vector, so age-proofing and source-verification are non-negotiable, first-order brand controls.

Risk Analysis

Operational Risks

  • Staff no-shows and attrition, skill mismatch, and the difficulty of supervising quality at scale — mitigated by a replacement SLA, a trained bench, QC/mystery-audits and area supervisors.

Financial Risks

  • GST-on-gross and EPF/ESIC/gratuity load if employing; a long CAC payback; and low tolerance for staff churn — mitigated by pricing the retainer to absorb compliance, a hybrid employ/broker structure at the low tier, and retention bonuses.

Regulatory Risks

  • Placement-agency licensing (the Delhi model may spread), a DPDP breach (up to ₹250 crore), and minimum-wage attachment — mitigated by licence discipline, DPDP-by-design, and specialist counsel on the employ-vs-broker structure.

Reputational Risks

  • Staff theft or harm at a client home, any trafficking/child-labour exposure, or a worker-rights backlash (as Urban Company faced) — mitigated by rigorous BGV, fidelity/theft insurance, strict age-proofing, and a proactively pro-worker, fair-wage, registered-employment narrative.

Scalability Risks

  • The high-touch model resists blitz-scaling and the trained-pool is a bottleneck — mitigated by the academy as a supply engine, disciplined city-by-city expansion, and technology for operational leverage.

The Fee Structure the Market Uses — and How This Concept Changes It

The market norm is a one-time commission of one month's salary (50% at registration, balance on joining) plus a 6-month free replacement, with the salary paid directly to the worker. BookMyBai charges exactly this — 1 month's salary + 18% GST. The innovation here is to convert that one-time transaction into a recurring managed retainer, adding payroll, supervision, compliance and an ongoing guarantee.

Revenue Model — Four Streams

  • Placement fee — one-time, 1–1.5 months of staff salary (a premium over the market's 1 month, justified by training, vetting and the guarantee).
  • Monthly management retainer — the recurring core: ~₹3,000–8,000 per placed staff per month, or a household bundle of ~₹15,000–40,000/monthEST., covering payroll processing, supervision/QC, the replacement guarantee and compliance.
  • Payroll-processing margin — under employer-of-record, a spread on salary administration (which also triggers GST-on-gross, priced accordingly).
  • Training academy — fees from staff (or amortised into placement) plus potential third-party/corporate training revenue.

Retention Strategy

Retention is the model, because the product is a recurring retainer. The levers are the replacement guarantee (which removes the single biggest reason to churn), quarterly household reviews, a dedicated relationship manager, staff-loyalty and retention bonuses (so the client's staff don't leave), and the switching cost of a managed, compliant, payroll-integrated relationship that is genuinely painful to unwind.

Cost Structure

This is a labour-and-operations business: the dominant cost lines are ops/supervisor and recruiter headcount, the academy, BGV and compliance. Under the recommended employer-of-record lane, EPF/ESIC/gratuity and GST-on-gross are real additional loads — deliberately absorbed into retainer pricing because that compliance is precisely what a family cannot replicate through informal hiring.

Pricing Strategy

Priced on trust and risk removal, not on the underlying salary (which is pass-through). The placement fee sits modestly above the market's one-month norm, justified by training and the guarantee; the recurring retainer is the value engine and the switching cost. Acute setup moments (a returning NRI furnishing a home, a founder post-exit) carry the lowest price sensitivity.

Section 09

USP & Moat

The market sells a one-time match and disappears. This business sells the one thing the informal market cannot: someone who stays accountable.

Why Families Choose This Over WhatsApp and Agencies

  • Someone else is accountable. If the cook quits on Tuesday, a trained replacement arrives — no scramble. Staff are pre-trained and background-verified; payroll, PF and compliance are handled; a single relationship manager owns the household. The informal market offers none of this, and existing agencies offer a one-time match and then vanish.
  • Trained, not just sourced. The academy produces certified staff — continental cooking, childcare, elder-care, discretion/security — a genuinely different product from a listings-page match.
  • A guarantee backed by a bench. The replacement SLA is only credible because the academy and scale make holding a trained bench economic — which is exactly why a thin placement agency cannot copy it.

Why Competitors Can't Easily Copy It

  • The trained-staff pool + academy is a proprietary, certified supply that takes years and capital to build — a placement agency cannot conjure it from a listings database.
  • The replacement guarantee is only credible if a bench exists, which only the academy plus scale make economic — a structural, not cosmetic, barrier.
  • Trust and brand in a vacuum. Reputation within a small, referral-dense UHNI community compounds over time and cannot be bought with a marketing budget.
  • The management / employer-of-record layer — supervision, QC, EPF/ESIC/DPDP compliance — is genuine operational work, not a feature toggle, and it is the barrier the gig players (whose DNA is cheap, fast and high-frequency) would have to rebuild an entire business to clear.
  • Family-office pedigree (Aamara) gives first-party UHNI distribution and credibility a startup cannot replicate.
Section 10

FAQ — Questions an Investment Committee Will Ask

1. If your placed staff steals from or harms a client, who's liable?
The business carries fidelity/theft insurance and bonding, runs rigorous BGV (criminal check plus police verification), and uses an employer-of-record structure that lets it act — suspend, replace, pursue — rather than leaving the family exposed. Liability is contractually bounded and insured, and the guarantee triggers an immediate vetted replacement. (The specific Indian fidelity-cover product is to be confirmed with an underwriter during setup.)
2. Isn't this just a placement agency with a nicer website?
No. A placement agency makes a one-time match and disappears; it is judged on fill rate. This business employs or supervises the staff, runs payroll and compliance, does QC, and guarantees replacement — with roughly 60%+ of revenue as recurring retainer, not one-off fees. The two closest players prove the gap: EliteButlers (premium) stops at recruitment, and BookMyBai (scale) stops at a 6-month guarantee — neither actually manages.
3. How do you stop clients poaching your trained staff and dropping the retainer?
Four locks. First, the retainer's value is ongoing — payroll, compliance, replacement, QC — not just the person. Second, a contractual non-solicit plus a buy-out clause. Third, staff loyalty is to the company (career path, PF, bonuses, academy), not just the household. Fourth, if a family goes direct, they lose the guarantee — the exact risk they were paying to avoid. This loop is the thesis's central sensitivity and is what the Mumbai pilot exists to prove.
4. How do you stop your trained staff being poached by rivals or other families?
Registered employment with PF and benefits, retention bonuses, real career progression (maid → cook → estate manager), and academy certification that is worth more inside the system than outside it. Managed, formal employment beats informal cash on stability — the thing a good cook or estate manager most lacks today.
5. The informal market undercuts you — a bai costs ₹18k, so why pay your retainer?
Because the business doesn't compete on the salary — that's pass-through. It sells risk removal to households for whom a bad hire, a theft, or a sudden quit costs far more than a retainer. This is a trust product for the top 1%, not a price product; the buyer is comparing the retainer to the cost of getting it wrong, not to the cost of a cheaper maid.
6. If staff churn is high, do the unit economics survive?
Churn is the key sensitivity, and the model is designed around it. The academy and bench convert churn from a client crisis into a routine fulfilment event, and retainer revenue is per-household not per-person, so a replacement doesn't lose the account. Retention bonuses and career pathways attack the root cause. LTV is stress-tested at 25–40% annual staff churn.
7. How big can this get — isn't it inherently un-scalable and high-touch?
It is deliberately city-by-city, not blitz-scale. Technology (CRM, family app, ops tooling) provides leverage; the academy is the scalable supply engine; and adjacencies (elder-care, concierge, corporate guest-houses) extend ARPU per household. The model optimises for margin and moat, not land-grab — the opposite of the gig players.
8. Snabbit and Pronto raised $100M+ — won't they crush you by moving upmarket?
Their DNA, unit economics and brand are cheap, fast and high-frequency. Premium trust, trained live-in staff and employer-of-record management is a different business they would have to rebuild and re-brand for — and moving downmarket-to-premium is one of the hardest transitions in services. Their funding validates the category, not this niche.
9. Could the coming domestic-worker law kill the model?
The opposite. The draft National Policy targets placement-agency regulation and model contracts — a formal, compliant, DPDP-ready, registered-employment operator is the intended survivor, while informal players get squeezed. This business is built for the regime that is coming, not the one that is fading.
10. Employ vs. broker — which, and why does it matter to us?
Employer-of-record (hybrid at the lower tier). It is heavier — EPF/ESIC/gratuity, GST-on-gross — but it is what makes the trust, control and guarantee real, and it is the moat. The retainer is priced to absorb the compliance, and that compliance is precisely what a family cannot replicate through informal hiring.
11. What's your defensibility once someone copies the playbook?
The academy-built trained pool, the guarantee-enabling bench, and reputation in a tiny, referral-dense community all compound with time — plus Aamara's first-party UHNI distribution. Copying the website is easy; copying two years of trained supply and accumulated trust is not.
12. What about a trafficking, child-labour or worker-exploitation scandal?
These are treated as hard red lines and first-order brand risks. No under-14s (child domestic work is a notified hazardous, prohibited occupation), documented age-proofing and source-verification, registered fair-wage employment, and a proactively pro-worker narrative — the deliberate anti-Urban-Company positioning — are built in from day one, not bolted on.
13. What's the single biggest risk?
The informal-market undercut and direct-poaching loop at scale — a family takes the trained staff and drops the retainer. Every element above (ongoing retainer value, non-solicit plus buy-out, staff loyalty via formal employment, loss-of-guarantee) is designed to break that loop, but it is the central sensitivity of the thesis and must be proven in the Mumbai pilot before any capital goes into expansion.
