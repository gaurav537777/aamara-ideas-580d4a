Section 01

Executive Summary

India is minting new liquid wealth faster than almost any economy on earth — but the plumbing to administer that wealth is a decade behind the money.

Family Office as a Service is a Mumbai-first, recurring-fee business that runs the non-discretionary admin and concierge layer around a wealthy household's financial life: consolidated cross-asset reporting, bookkeeping and bill-pay, tax and CA coordination, a secure document vault, estate and succession logistics, insurance review, next-generation financial education, and lifestyle concierge. It is built for India's ₹25–200 crore "emerging-affluent / lower-UHNI" band — households too small to justify a bespoke single-family office (which globally needs ₹800 crore-plus in assets to pay for its own staff), yet actively product-pushed and under-served by private banks. Critically, the model is priced on a fixed retainer, never as a percentage of assets, and it is deliberately kept separate from discretionary money management — making it structurally conflict-free in a market where almost everyone else earns from the products they sell.

The legal spine, stated up front. This entity is not a SEBI Registered Investment Adviser (RIA). Pure administration, factual consolidated reporting, bill-pay, document custody, and the coordination of the client's own CA, lawyer and adviser are not "investment advice in securities" under the SEBI (Investment Advisers) Regulations, 2013. The RIA fixed-fee cap of ₹1.51 lakh per family per year therefore does not apply — the retainer is uncapped — precisely because the business does not recommend securities for a fee. Any actual securities advice or money management is referred out to the client's own RIA/PMS. This partition is the design's backbone and is enforced in every contract and marketing asset (see Macro & Regulatory and USP & Moat).

The problem is structural, not cyclical. India's ultra-high-net-worth (US$30M+) population rose 63% between 2021 and 2026, from roughly 12,000 to 19,877 — now the world's sixth-largest — yet the country had only about 300 family offices in 2024, up from 45 in 2018 (PwC/EY). Wealth is out-running the servicing infrastructure by roughly a decade. Today a newly-liquid founder stitches their financial life together across a CA, a broker, a private-bank relationship manager and a spreadsheet — with no single, conflict-free "quarterback." That the incumbents are conflicted is not an accusation but a documented fact: over 57% of Indian bank relationship managers admitted mis-selling products to hit targets (Oct-2024 survey), and banks earn 65–70% first-year commission on in-house insurance. In a category whose entire value is trust, that is the opening.

The solution is a senior, human-led services team sitting on top of a bought-in reporting platform, delivering one consolidated view and one accountable point of contact — agnostic to who manages the money. The moat is the sticky, data-rich, trust-based relationship that product-pushing banks structurally cannot credibly offer, and that percentage-of-assets incumbents find uneconomic to serve at flat fees below their AUM math.

Target customers span four personas, each captured at a moment of need: the recently-exited SaaS founder (cash-rich, admin-poor); the senior technology executive with concentrated cross-border ESOP/RSU holdings; the second-generation inheritor who wants stewardship without becoming a CFO (only 7% of Indian heirs feel obligated to run the family business); and the returning NRI juggling two financial lives across FEMA and multi-currency reporting.

Why now: liquidity events are structural, not episodic — over US$1.8 billion of startup employee liquidity has flowed across 100+ Indian startups since 2020, including ₹1,448 crore across ~23 companies in 2024 alone; the wealth-management asset pool is projected to more than double from ~$1.1 trillion (FY24) to $2.3 trillion (FY29); a great wealth transfer of roughly ₹108 lakh crore is beginning over the coming decade; and both SEBI (Dec-2024) and the RBI (mis-selling directions from Jan-2027) are actively tilting the field toward fee-based, conflict-free advice. The window to plant a clean, flat-fee flag is open now.

Key highlights: an estimatedEST. ₹3.5–6 crore Year-1 cash requirement; a defensible 5-year revenue targetEST. of ₹24–120 crore at 300–1,500 families; break-evenEST. at roughly 75–90 families; very high structural retention (>90%EST.) because switching means re-plumbing an entire financial life; and a phased Mumbai + Bengaluru → Delhi/Hyderabad/Pune → NRI-hub expansion path. The single largest execution risk is not market demand — it is the Aamara conflict-of-interest challenge, addressed structurally (separate entity, arms-length, contractual no-cross-sell) rather than verbally.

The Load-Bearing Distinction — Who We Actually Serve

Tier discipline matters here. The target band is ₹25–200 crore net worth (≈ US$3M–$24M), which sits below the Knight Frank US$30M UHNWI cutoff. So the market cannot be sized off the 19,877 UHNWI number — that segment is above the target. The addressable base is the top of the US$10M+ HNWI pool (85,698) plus the US$3–10M affluent tier beneath it. This band is precisely the one that is too small for a bespoke single-family office yet too complex, and too product-pushed, to be well-served by a private bank.

Consumer & Decision-Maker Behaviour

This is a trust-and-timing market. The buyer is financially sophisticated on earning money but often a novice at administering it, and the purchase is almost always triggered by an event — an exit, an ESOP buyback, an inheritance, a return from abroad — not steady-state planning. Acquisition must therefore be relationship-led and timed to the liquidity moment (via VCs, investment bankers, CA networks and ESOP-liquidity platforms), and the first proof point must be delivered fast: a single consolidated net-worth view the client has never had before.

The Regulatory Line — The Core Legal Design

Why the flat retainer is legal and uncapped. The SEBI (Investment Advisers) Regulations, 2013 (as amended to Dec-2024) govern "investment advice in securities." Pure administration, factual consolidated reporting, bookkeeping, bill-pay, document custody, tax/CA/lawyer coordination, estate logistics, insurance review, education and concierge are not securities advice. The RIA fixed-fee cap of ₹1.51 lakh per family per year (and the 2.5%-of-AUA cap) binds only a registered adviser recommending securities. Because this business does not recommend securities for a fee, those caps do not apply, and the ₹3–25 lakh retainer is lawful.
  • The structural partition (enforced operationally): In-house, uncapped flat fee — reporting, bill-pay, vault, tax/estate coordination, insurance review, concierge, education, project-managing the client's advisers. Referred out — any actual recommendation of securities, or discretionary money management, goes to the client's own RIA/PMS/CA/lawyer.
  • Discipline of language: "advice-coordination" means project-managing the client's CA/lawyer/RIA — it is not giving advice. This wording must be rigorous in every client contract and marketing asset, or the RIA line blurs and the fee-cap risk returns.
  • Optional escalation structure: if securities advice is ever brought in-house, do it through a separately-registered non-individual RIA subsidiary charging within the ₹1.51 lakh cap — kept legally and economically distinct from the (uncapped) admin entity.
  • "Family office" is unregulated per se: the term appears in no Indian statute, and in October 2025 SEBI expressly clarified it is not examining a dedicated framework. Structures are assembled from the Indian Trusts Act 1882, Companies Act 2013, LLP Act 2008, Income-tax Act 1961, FEMA 1999 and applicable SEBI regs.
  • DPDP Act 2023 + Rules 2025: holding highly sensitive financial data makes the business a Data Fiduciary — likely a Significant Data Fiduciary at scale, requiring an India-based DPO, independent audits and DPIAs. Penalties up to ₹250 crore per breach, with no cure period; hard compliance deadline 13 May 2027. This is the single biggest compliance cost and risk — and, delivered well, a selling point.
  • AML/KYC: bill-pay and vault touch money movement, so robust onboarding KYC/AML is required.
  • Tax & estate: tax coordination (managing the client's CA) is unregulated; return preparation would fall under CA/ERI norms, so filing stays with the client's CA. Wills under the Indian Succession Act 1925, probate/succession certificates via courts, private trusts under the Trusts Act 1882, HUF planning — all coordination, executed by the client's lawyer.
  • RBI mis-selling directions (from 1-Jan-2027): tighten product-push by banks — a direct tailwind for the conflict-free model, and a reminder to keep the business's own conduct spotless.

Risk Analysis

Strategic & Reputational Risks

  • The Aamara conflict-of-interest challenge — the single biggest risk. A PMS/AIF manager building a "conflict-free" admin layer invites the charge that it merely funnels clients into Aamara's own products. The only credible defence is structural, not verbal: a separate legal entity, arms-length, a contractual no-cross-sell rule, and a product-agnostic mandate. If that firewall is soft, the entire flat-fee/conflict-free USP — the whole moat — collapses.
  • Scope-creep across the RIA line if relationship managers start informally "recommending" securities — mitigated by language discipline, training and contract design.

Regulatory Risks

  • A future SEBI family-office framework (currently disclaimed) could impose registration or conduct rules.
  • DPDP enforcement or a data breach — high-stakes given concentrated sensitive data (₹250 cr/breach, no cure period, 13-May-2027 deadline).

Financial & Scalability Risks

  • People-heavy cost base (60–70% personnel) means margins are negative until ~50 families and break-even needs ~75–90 families — a real runway requirement before operating leverage appears.
  • Talent scarcity: the model depends on senior, credentialed hires (ex-private-bank, CAs, CFPs) who are expensive and poachable.
  • An economic downturn that compresses exits/ESOP events slows the refill of the target band.

Trust & Content

  • Thought leadership on conflict-free administration — publishing the plain-English case against product-push wealth management, aimed at newly-liquid founders who don't yet know what they don't know.

The Aamara Ecosystem (arms-length)

Aamara's existing relationships and credibility are a genuine head start — used strictly at arms-length and product-agnostically, so the conflict-free promise stays intact (see USP & Moat and FAQ).

Retention Strategy

Trust is the product, and retention is structural: once the consolidated data, bill-pay rails and document vault live with us, switching means re-plumbing an entire financial life. The active work of retention is therefore service quality — senior, credentialed people; first-class DPDP-grade security; transparent reporting; and visible, proactive value between the once-a-year "moments" (a succession draft delivered, an insurance overhaul completed, a tax deadline handled).

USP & Moat

In a market where nearly everyone is paid to touch the money, being paid not to is both the differentiator and the defence.

Why Clients Choose This Over Banks, MFOs and Wealthtech

  • Flat-fee, conflict-free vs % AUM incumbents. The business cannot be bribed by product commissions or grow its fee by inflating AUM. That is a structural trust advantage the mis-selling channel — 57% of RMs admitting mis-selling, 65–70% insurance commissions — can never credibly claim.
  • The admin layer is the sticky relationship. Product-push banks and even fee-only advisers don't run a client's life-admin. Once we hold the consolidated data, bill-pay rails and document vault, we are embedded — the switching cost is re-plumbing an entire financial life, which is why retention runs above 90%.
  • Agnostic to who manages the money. Because we don't manage assets, we can sit above every product provider and be the client's neutral quarterback — a position no one selling a product can occupy.
  • Regulatory positioning as a feature. Being deliberately not an RIA lets us charge an uncapped flat retainer while staying clean — a design % AUM firms and capped RIAs can't easily replicate without cannibalising their core.

Why Competitors Can't Easily Copy It

  • The innovator's dilemma protects us. 360 ONE, Kotak or Nuvama can bolt on a "family-office desk," but they cannot credibly go flat-fee and product-agnostic without cannibalising the % AUM and distribution revenue that is their core. Their DNA is product; ours is admin.
  • Data and switching costs compound. The longitudinal, cross-asset dataset we accumulate for each family gets more valuable — and more painful to migrate — every year. A well-funded copycat cannot instantly manufacture years of a client's consolidated financial history.
  • Trust is built, not bought. In a category where the product is trust, a brand takes years of consistent, conflict-free delivery to establish — capital alone doesn't shortcut it.
  • The Aamara adjacency, kept clean, is an asset rivals lack — credibility, ecosystem deal-flow and operational know-how — provided the arms-length firewall holds (see FAQ 1). The firewall is existential, not cosmetic: the moment the business soft-pushes Aamara products, the conflict-free USP dies.
Section 10

FAQ — Questions an Investment Committee Will Ask

1. Doesn't this conflict with Aamara's own PMS/AIF? Isn't "conflict-free" hypocritical?
This is the single most important question, and the answer must be structural, not verbal. The admin entity is a separate legal company, arms-length, product-agnostic, with a contractual no-cross-selling rule. It is compensated only by the client's flat retainer and does not steer clients into Aamara products. If a client independently chooses Aamara's PMS, that is disclosed and at the client's own initiative. The moment the business soft-pushes Aamara, the entire "conflict-free" USP — the whole moat — dies. So the firewall is existential, and it is enforced through legal structure and a written charter, not a promise.
2. Why would a founder pay a flat fee when private banking is "free"?
Because "free" private banking is paid for invisibly — through the 65–70% first-year commissions on in-house products and the documented 57% of relationship managers who admit mis-selling to hit targets. The founder pays far more, in worse products, without seeing the bill. A transparent flat fee is both cheaper in the round and aligned — the adviser earns the same whatever the client buys.
3. How do you make money without AUM?
Through tiered flat retainers (₹3–25 lakh) plus à-la-carte project fees, on a base with high retention (>90%EST.), low referral-led CAC, and an estimated LTV:CAC above 20:1. The model doesn't need assets under management — it needs families and stickiness, both of which it has structurally.
4. Where exactly is the regulatory line between advice and administration?
The business does not recommend securities, so it is not a SEBI RIA, so the ₹1.51 lakh fixed-fee cap does not apply. It administers, reports and coordinates the client's own CA, lawyer and adviser. Any actual securities advice is referred out — or, if ever brought in-house, run through a separately-registered, separately-capped RIA subsidiary. The distinction is enforced by disciplined contract and marketing language ("advice-coordination," not "advice").
5. You hold everything — what happens if you're breached?
Data security is treated as a board-level priority and a selling point, not an afterthought. The build is DPDP-grade from Day 1: India-based DPO, encryption at rest and in transit, independent audits, DPIAs and a breach-response protocol. The DPDP Act's exposure — up to ₹250 crore per breach with no cure period, hard deadline 13 May 2027 — is exactly why security is engineered in from the first family, not retrofitted.
6. Can't 360 ONE, Kotak or Nuvama just copy this?
They can add a "family-office desk," but they cannot credibly go flat-fee and product-agnostic without cannibalising the % AUM and distribution revenue that funds them — a textbook innovator's dilemma. Their institutional DNA is product distribution; this business's is administration. Copying the positioning without changing the revenue model would be immediately visible to a skeptical client.
7. What if the client wants you to manage their money too?
We refer to vetted RIAs/PMS — including, on the client's own initiative and with full disclosure, Aamara — while staying the neutral quarterback. Managing money ourselves would forfeit the conflict-free moat, so it is a deliberate, permanent line, not a stepping stone.
8. Is the market really big enough at flat fees?
Yes, on a bottom-up basis. SAM is an estimatedEST. ₹3,000 crore/year (families × retainer); break-even needs only ~75–90 families; a Year-3 target of ~300 families is ~₹24 crore of revenue, and a 5-year path toward ~1,500 families is ~₹120 crore. The band — the top of the 85,698 HNWI pool plus the affluent tier below — is large and genuinely under-served.
9. Isn't this just a people business with no scalability?
Early on, yes — 60–70% of cost is people. But the shared platform and central team create real operating leverage past ~40–50 families, and the data moat plus >90% retention turn it into a compounding annuity rather than a project-services treadmill. The scalability comes from stickiness and leverage on a fixed senior base, not from thin, transactional volume.
10. What's the real churn risk?
Fee competition is not the primary risk, because switching cost is structural (re-plumbing a financial life). The real risk is a service-quality lapse — a missed deadline, a reporting error, a security scare. Mitigation is senior talent, SLA-bound coordination workflows, and disciplined proactive communication between the annual "moments."
11. What if SEBI decides to regulate family offices?
SEBI expressly clarified in October 2025 that it is not pursuing a dedicated family-office framework. If that changes, the business is a lightly-exposed services company (LLP/Pvt Ltd) and can register a subsidiary if a specific activity ever requires it. The core admin/coordination work sits outside securities regulation by design.
12. Why now, and not five years ago?
Because the tailwinds only recently aligned: the wealth pool is doubling to $2.3 trillion by FY29, ESOP/exit liquidity has become structural (over $1.8 billion since 2020), a ~₹108 lakh crore great transfer is beginning, and the RBI's 2027 mis-selling crackdown is about to squeeze the product-push banks. Five years ago the band was too thin and the regulatory wind was neutral; the window is open now.
13. What's the strategic value / exit?
A trusted, data-rich, high-retention relationship with hundreds or thousands of newly-liquid families is strategically valuable to any wealth manager, bank or global multi-family office — and, standalone, it is a durable, defensible annuity. The very stickiness that protects the business also makes the book of relationships an asset others would pay a premium to acquire.
