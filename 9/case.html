Section 01

Executive Summary

India has minted a fortune in second homes and NRI-owned flats — and left their care to a cottage economy of relatives, watchmen and freelance caretakers nobody is accountable to.

Second-Home & NRI Property Management is a Mumbai-first managed service that keeps a second home or an NRI-owned flat guest-ready, secure and well-maintained — housekeeping and upkeep, preventive maintenance, security and inspections, guest-readiness, staff supervision, bill/utility/society management, and NRI tax/compliance coordination — with transparent, photographic, app-level reporting. Rental-yield management is offered as an option, not the business model. Revenue is a monthly retainer per home + a commission on managed rentals + à-la-carte project work: recurring, asset-light, and scalable cluster by cluster across Mumbai, Alibaug, Lonavala/Khandala and Goa.

The problem is a structural conflict of interest in the existing market. The scaled players a wealthy owner has heard of — StayVista, Lohono, SaffronStays, Elivaas — are rental operators: they earn only when your home is let, on undisclosed 25–40% revenue-shares, so their incentive is occupancy, not the care of your asset. At the other end sits a low-trust informal layer of caretakers and relatives that NoBroker itself describes as prone to "poor maintenance, inadequate tenant screening, and a lack of regular updates," with vacant properties exposed to "unauthorised occupation, forged documentation, and ownership disputes." Between the two, only one small player (HomeShikari) publishes a clean fixed care retainer — and it is a three-city South-India tenancy product, not a premium coastal second-home service. The owner-first box is empty.

Why now. Real estate is now India's #1 preferred asset — 63% of buyers favour it over stocks, gold and FDs (ANAROCK H1 2025) — and 62% of HNIs plan to buy property in the next 12–24 months, with 54% eyeing hill/beach destinations and 25% already owning a holiday home (India Sotheby's 2025). NRIs invested roughly $13.1 billion in Indian real estate in FY21 and their share of purchases has climbed from ~7–10% to ~18–20%, with DLF alone reporting NRIs at 23% of FY24 sales. Alibaug tourist footfall nearly doubled to 4.5 million. And VC-backed villa operators running 1,000+ property portfolios have already proven that owners will hand over the keys to a managed operator — validating the category while leaving the owner's side of the table wide open.

The solution is a fiduciary, owner-aligned service: a retainer to protect the asset, backed by verified and bonded local staff, insurance, IoT security, tamper-evident inventory, and an owner app whose time-stamped photographic proof directly attacks the buyer's number-one fear — theft and neglect of a home they cannot see. For NRIs, the friction they most dread (31.2% rental TDS, FEMA repatriation, society disputes) becomes the service's highest-value add-on rather than the owner's headache.

Target customers are four personas: the Mumbai "weekend baron" with an Alibaug villa; the "remote landlord" NRI with a rented-out Mumbai flat; the retiree with a Lonavala home who wants pure upkeep and no tenants; and the "yield investor" with a Goa holiday-let who wants occupancy and clean statements.

Key highlights: an estimatedEST. ₹1.5–3.0 crore Year-1 seed requirement; a conservative Year-3 revenue base caseEST. of ₹4–10 crore at 300–500 homes under management (with realistic captured economics implying ₹9–25 crore); break-evenEST. at roughly 100–130 homes; estimatedEST. LTV:CAC of 15–40:1 on referral-led acquisition; and a Mumbai+Alibaug → Lonavala/Nashik → Goa → NRI-metro expansion path. The single biggest risk is theft or staff misconduct at a client home — a trust the entire model is sold on, destroyable by one incident — which is why bonded staff, insurance and IoT must be day-one, not a later add-on.

Consumer & Decision-Maker Behaviour

The buyer is emotionally and financially invested in an asset they physically cannot supervise — an NRI eight time zones away, or a Mumbai owner who visits an Alibaug villa 30–50 nights a year. They are price-insensitive on care but unforgiving on reliability, and the purchase is triggered either at handover of a new second home or after a bad experience with an informal caretaker. Acquisition is therefore best made at the point of purchase (through developers and brokers) or through trusted-advisor referral (wealth managers, CAs, NRI banking) — the channels already present in the owner's life.

Section 04

Macro & Regulatory

The single most favourable regulatory fact: pure rental and care management does not trigger RERA — agent registration is mandatory only for sale/resale brokerage. The business registers under RERA only if it brokers sales. The live risks lie elsewhere: CRZ (coastal demolition exposure), NRI TDS mechanics, and DPDP data protection.

PESTLE Analysis

Political / Legal

RERA, FEMA, the Income-Tax Act, GST, state tourism acts, CRZ and DPDP are all live — and broadly supportive of formalisation, which favours a compliant branded operator over informal caretakers.

Economic

Rising HNI/NRI wealth, record remittances and a durable post-COVID second-home boom underpin willingness to pay for care (see Market & Industry).

Social

Hybrid work and "workations", the status value of a curated second home, and the diaspora's deep emotional tie to Indian property all drive demand for reliable, remote-managed upkeep.

Technological

Owner apps, IoT security and dynamic pricing have normalised — the substrate for a transparent, photographic, app-reported service exists off the shelf.

Environmental

CRZ and coastal fragility around Alibaug and Goa, plus the monsoon upkeep cycle, are both a risk (demolition, damp) and a recurring-demand driver (monsoon-proofing is peak care season).

Regulatory rates

18% GST on the management fee; 31.2% TDS on NRI rent; 12.5% LTCG on NRI property sale (post-Jul 2024); 5%/18% GST on short-stay hospitality (2025 change) — detailed below.

Government Regulations & Compliance

  • RERA — agent registration is mandatory for sale/resale brokerage (MahaRERA ₹10k individual / ₹1 lakh firm; Goa RERA similar), but pure rental/care management does not trigger it. Register only if brokering sales.
  • FEMA (NRI ownership) — NRIs may freely buy residential/commercial property but cannot buy agricultural land, plantations or farmhouses except by inheritance — a real diligence flag for Alibaug/Goa "farmhouse" plots. Repatriation is capped at US$1 million/financial year from an NRO account (Forms 15CA/15CB), with NRE/FCNR funds fully repatriable.
  • TDS on NRI rent (Section 195) — 31.2% with no threshold; the tenant needs a TAN and files Form 27Q. Managing this, plus securing a Lower-TDS (Form 13) certificate, is a major NRI value-add.
  • TDS on NRI property sale (Section 195) — LTCG is now a flat 12.5% without indexation (post-23 Jul 2024), on full consideration, with surcharge/cess up to ~14.95%. The Lower-TDS certificate is again the value lever.
  • GST — 18% on the management fee/commission (SAC 997221). On the rental arm, short-stay rooms ≤₹7,500/night are 5% (no ITC) from 22 Sep 2025 (down from 12%); >₹7,500/night are 18% with ITC.
  • Homestay/B&B registration — Maharashtra (MTDC) and Goa (Goa Registration of Tourist Trade Act 1982) registration is required if operating short-stays, with mandatory guest registers — a compliance-as-a-service opportunity for owners.
  • CRZ (coastal) — the CRZ Notification 2019 imposes no-development zones of 50m (dense CRZ-III A) to 200m (sparse III B) from the high-tide line, with CRZ-I no-build and real demolition risk. This is the highest execution risk for Alibaug/Goa coastal homes, mitigated by CRZ diligence at onboarding and disclaiming construction advice.
  • Society bye-laws & gram panchayat — Maharashtra caps non-occupancy charges at 10% of service charges and requires registered leave-and-licence; Alibaug/Goa village homes fall under gram panchayat rather than co-op society. Managing this liaison is part of the service.
  • DPDP Act 2023 — owner KYC and guest passport data require consent and safeguards; penalties up to ₹250 crore, with full compliance by 13 May 2027. The app must be DPDP-compliant from day one.

Risk Analysis — Top Six

  1. CRZ / illegal-construction exposure (High) — managing a home later demolished, or advising on a CRZ-I property. Mitigant: CRZ diligence at onboarding; never give construction advice.
  2. Theft / staff misconduct / owner liability (High) — the single biggest trust-killer. Mitigant: bonded/verified staff, insurance, tamper-evident inventory, IoT cameras, dual-control on cash.
  3. NRI tax/FEMA missteps (Medium–High) — mishandled TDS triggers owner penalties. Mitigant: a partner CA network; never touch owner money without 15CA/CB compliance.
  4. Labour reliability in thin markets (Medium–High) — Alibaug/Goa caretaker scarcity and poaching. Mitigant: employ core staff, pay a wage premium, run a training academy, deploy roving supervisors.
  5. Regulatory reprice (Medium) — GST/TDS both moved in 2024–25. Mitigant: pass-through pricing and active compliance monitoring.
  6. Reputational single-point failure (Medium) — one viral theft or damage story. Mitigant: SLA guarantees, insurance and a rehearsed incident playbook.

Trust, the transparency app and local density together create switching cost. Technology Stack

Owner App — the Core Trust Device

Time-stamped inspection reports with photos and video, service logs, a preventive-maintenance calendar, and issue tickets with resolution SLAs — turning an opaque, anxious arrangement into visible, photographic proof for an owner who cannot be there.

Booking calendar, dynamic-pricing suggestions, a guest-readiness checklist, and transparent rental statements with payout tracking (into an NRO account for NRIs) — the option, delivered with performance transparency.

Financials & Compliance

Bill/utility/society payment tracking, à-la-carte project quotes and approvals, and an annual owner statement with TDS/GST breakup — the NRI compliance friction turned into a managed service.

Cameras, smart locks and water-leak/humidity sensors (coastal monsoon protection), plus geo-tagged staff attendance and inventory tags for theft deterrence — the hardware backbone of the trust promise.

Ops & Dispatch Tooling

Staff rostering, roving-supervisor routing, vendor coordination and an incident playbook — the machinery that lets one supervisor reliably cover a cluster of homes.

DPDP-Compliant Data Handling

Consent-based owner KYC and guest passport data with encryption and audit trails from day one — a legal requirement (₹250 crore penalty ceiling) and, delivered transparently, a trust signal.

Revenue Model

Three streams, deliberately anchored on the recurring retainer rather than rental. The care retainer is the standalone base product and the higher-margin one; the rental commission is pure upside for owners who want it, priced below the rental operators' norm to stay owner-friendly; and à-la-carte projects (monsoon-proofing, deep cleans, renovations) add episodic, high-markup revenue. Roughly a third of the target base (retirees, private-use HNIs) will never rent — and are among the stickiest, highest-margin customers.

Cost Structure

The dominant line is field supervision headcount — the roving supervisors whose density is the moat — followed by app/tech, insurance and referral fees. Because vendor spend (repairs, staff wages on managed homes) is largely pass-through, working capital discipline matters, but the fixed-cost base is modest and asset-light, which is what makes ~100–130 homes a realistic break-even.

Pricing Strategy

Priced on protection and reliability, not on rental take. The fixed care retainer is transparent (against the opaque revenue-shares of rental operators), the rental commission is deliberately set below StayVista's 30%, and projects are cost-plus. This is the pricing expression of the owner-first positioning — the customer always knows what they pay and why, and the operator never earns more by pushing their home onto the rental market.

Section 09

USP & Moat

The rental operators earn only when your home is let. This business earns to keep it safe — which is exactly the promise they cannot make without breaking their own model.

Why Owners Choose This Over the Rental Operators and the Informal Market

  • Owner-first, not occupancy-first. StayVista, Lohono and SaffronStays make money only when your house is rented, on opaque 25–40% revenue-shares — their incentive is occupancy, not asset care. This business earns a retainer to protect the asset and shares rental upside only as an option, at a lower, transparent 20–25%. The incentive is aligned with the owner — a positioning moat competitors cannot copy without cannibalising their own model.
  • Trust and transparency as the product. Verified and bonded staff, insurance, tamper-evident inventory and app-level photographic proof directly attack the buyer's number-one fear — theft and neglect of a home they cannot watch.
  • Care without the rental strings. For the ~third of owners who never want tenants (retirees, private-use HNIs), the standalone care retainer is exactly what the rental-first market refuses to sell them.

Why Competitors Can't Easily Copy It

  • The fiduciary positioning is structurally closed to rental operators. A true protect-the-asset retainer conflicts with pushing rentals; for StayVista or Lohono to offer it credibly, they'd have to fight their own occupancy-based incentive and brand.
  • Local ops density is a physical moat. Winning a cluster — a building, a gated Alibaug community — lets one supervisor cover many homes, giving better unit economics and faster response than any national operator managing remotely can match. Density is earned home by home, not bought with capital.
  • Trust compounds and is slow to build. Reputation among a small, referral-dense community of HNI and NRI owners accumulates over years and cannot be shortcut with a marketing budget.
  • Compliance and reporting lock-in. Once the keys, the staff, the rental income and the NRI tax/FEMA rails all run through one operator, the switching cost per home rises every year.
Section 10

FAQ — Questions an Investment Committee Will Ask

1. How is this different from Isprava/StayVista/SaffronStays, who also "manage villas"?
They monetise via undisclosed 25–40% revenue-shares and earn only when your home is rented — their incentive is occupancy. This business is the owner's side of the table: a retainer to protect the home, with rental offered as an option at a lower, transparent 20–25%. Different customer promise, different incentive. StayVista's own 30% commission and the documented service complaints underline the gap.
2. What if the owner doesn't want to rent at all?
That is roughly a third of the target market — retirees and private-use HNIs — and it is the higher-margin one (~55–60% EST.). The care retainer is a complete standalone product; rental is pure upside, never the base case. This is the exact structural difference from the rental operators, who have nothing to sell an owner who won't let their home.
3. Owner trust — theft, damage, liability. How is this managed?
Trust is the product, so the controls are day-one, not later: bonded and verified staff, insurance, IoT cameras, tamper-evident inventory, dual-control on cash, SLA guarantees and a rehearsed incident playbook. NoBroker itself cites caretaker unreliability, theft and encroachment as the number-one NRI pain — this business is built to remove exactly that.
4. Isn't the revenue too seasonal?
Rental income is peaked (Alibaug Oct–May at 70–85% occupancy, with a monsoon lull), but the care retainer is flat — and the monsoon is peak care season (leaks, humidity, upkeep). Care revenue therefore counter-cyclically offsets rental seasonality, and the care-only flats carry no rental seasonality at all.
5. How do you get reliable local labour in Alibaug and Goa?
This is the hardest operational problem — thin markets, poaching, wage inflation. The answer is to employ a core roving-supervisor layer rather than rely on freelancers, pay a wage premium, run a training academy, and use geo-tagged attendance. Cluster density lets one supervisor cover many homes, which is what makes the wage premium affordable.
6. What are the unit economics per home?
EstimatedEST.: a rented Alibaug villa yields ~₹7.5 lakh gross revenue/year at ~40–50% margin; a care-only Mumbai flat ~₹2–2.5 lakh at ~55–60%. LTV:CAC is ~15–40:1 if referral-driven, given a 4–6 year hold typical of second homes. Retention is the key sensitivity.
7. NRI payment and FEMA friction — isn't that a barrier?
It is the opposite — it is the value-add. NRIs pay via NRO accounts, repatriation is capped at $1M/year with Forms 15CA/CB, and rent TDS is 31.2% under Section 195. Running those rails, plus securing a Lower-TDS (Form 13) certificate, is precisely the compliance service NRIs will pay a premium for rather than navigate alone.
8. Why won't NoBroker or Anarock just do this?
They are rent-percentage-driven long-let managers optimised for metro flats, not premium coastal second homes with staffed villas, guest-readiness and CRZ-grade local ops. Extending there means building new operational muscle for a different customer. It is possible, but it is not their focus — and the density and trust lead compounds while they decide.
9. Break-even — how many homes?
EstimatedEST. at ~100–130 homes under management against ~₹2.5–3 crore of fixed overhead, reachable within the Mumbai + Alibaug cluster by Year 2–3. The asset-light cost base is what keeps that threshold modest.
10. Isn't this just a glorified caretaker service that doesn't scale?
The informal caretaker is precisely the substitute this displaces. Scale comes from cluster density, app-standardised operations, and a repeatable city playbook — the same asset-light logic that took StayVista to ~1,000 homes and SaffronStays past 450. The difference is the owner-first promise on top of that operating model.
11. What are the regulatory landmines?
CRZ demolition risk on coastal homes, GST (18% on the fee; 5%/18% on rentals post-2025), NRI TDS (12.5% LTCG post-2024), and DPDP (₹250 crore penalties). All are manageable with diligence and CA/legal partners — and notably, RERA does not apply to pure management, only to sales brokerage.
12. What's the customer acquisition cost and channel?
Referral, broker and developer-led acquisition keeps CAC at an estimatedEST. ₹15,000–40,000/home, and the wealth-manager and NRI-network channels (natural for Aamara) compound. Because the model wins clusters, neighbour-to-neighbour referral within a building or community drives CAC down further over time.
13. What stops a well-funded rental operator from adding "owner care" and crushing you?
Their revenue model is occupancy-based, so a genuine fiduciary retainer conflicts with pushing rentals — adding it credibly means fighting their own incentive and brand. Meanwhile the trust and local-density lead is earned over time, home by home, not bought with capital. That is the defence, and it is why the positioning, not the feature set, is the moat.
